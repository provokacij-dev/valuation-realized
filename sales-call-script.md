# VALUATION REALIZED — Sales Call Script
## Based on Sam Ovens' Alchemic Conversion Script, customized for VR

Status: DRAFT (2026-03-21)

---

## Pre-Call Preparation (5 minutes before)

- Quiet room, no interruptions
- Phone with headphones, hands free for notes
- Record the call
- Have this script printed in front of you
- Pen and notepad ready
- Dim your screen or close laptop — be fully present
- **Release all attachment to the "YES." Put all attachment on "Accurate Diagnosis."** You are a Big Four M&A advisor. Your job is to diagnose, not to sell.
- Have their LinkedIn profile / company info pulled up (you should have researched before the call)
- Dial the moment the clock hits the minute

---

## Part One — Small Chat (1-2 minutes)

"Hey [Name], how's your week going?"

"Where are you calling from today?"

"Alright, well we can dive right in and get started with this call if you want?"

---

## Part Two — State Agenda & Take the Lead

"OK well how this call will go — I'll start by asking you some questions about your business and where you're at with your exit plans. Then if it sounds like I can help, and if it sounds like we're a good fit, I'll explain what I do and how that works. Then at the end you can make a decision whether it makes sense for you or not. Sound good?"

*(Wait for the "OK awesome" / "Sounds good" — then move forward.)*

---

## Part Three — Figure Out Why They're Here

"So [Name], tell me — what motivated you to take time out of your day and book this call with me?"

**Listen.** If they give a surface answer, probe deeper:
- "What do you mean by that?"
- "Tell me more about that..."
- "How long have you been thinking about this?"
- "What made you start thinking about an exit now specifically?"
- "Have you spoken to anyone else about this — a broker, an advisor, your accountant?"

**What you're listening for:** The real trigger. Often it's burnout, a life event, a partner conflict, a competitor who sold, an unsolicited offer they received, or simply "I've been building this for 15 years and I want to capture the value."

---

## Part Four — Understand Current Situation

"OK, tell me about your business. What does the company do?"

"How long have you been running it?"

"What's your annual revenue right now?" *(You MUST get a specific number before moving forward.)*

"And is that growing, flat, or declining?"

"How many employees do you have?"

"What's your role day-to-day? How involved are you in operations?"

"Do you have a management team that could run the business if you stepped back for three months?"

"How concentrated is your customer base? Does any single customer represent more than 15-20% of revenue?"

"Have you had your financials professionally audited or reviewed, or are you working off management accounts?"

"Has anyone — a buyer, a broker, a PE firm — ever approached you about acquiring the business?"

---

## Part Five — Gather Data & Understand the Pain

"OK [Name], so you're doing [revenue] a year. Where do you want this exit to land? What number would make this feel like it was worth everything you put in?"

*(Get a specific number. If they say "I don't know" — help them anchor:)*

"Well, businesses in your space typically trade at [X-Y]x EBITDA. Based on what you've told me, that puts you in the range of [$X to $Y]. Does that feel right to you, or were you hoping for something different?"

"OK and what's your motivation for exiting? What changes for you personally when this deal closes?"

"How would things be different for your family if you achieved that number?"

"And what would it mean if you went through the whole process — months of due diligence, lawyers, negotiations — and the deal fell apart? Or the final price came in 30% below what you expected?"

**In this part, support their vision.** If they're aiming too low, widen what's possible:

"You know, I've seen companies with your profile trade at significantly higher multiples — but only when the business is properly prepared for a buyer's evaluation. The difference between a 4x and a 6x multiple on your EBITDA could be [$ specific number]. That's the gap we're talking about."

---

## Part Six — Cause Pain (The Gap)

"OK [Name], so you want to exit at [$X]. Tell me — what's stopping you from just going to market tomorrow and getting that number?"

**Listen for the three magic statements:**
1. "I don't know how buyers evaluate businesses" — *Inability to do it alone*
2. "I want to make sure I don't leave money on the table" — *Wanting to do it right / faster*
3. "I've never sold a company before, I need someone who's been through this" — *Wanting a proven guide*

If they don't say these naturally, probe:
- "Have you ever sold a company before?"
- "Do you know what a buyer's due diligence process looks like from the inside?"
- "If a buyer sent you a 200-item due diligence request list tomorrow, would you be ready?"

---

## Part Seven — Releasing Control & Self-Admission

"OK [Name], so you're doing [$X] in revenue and you want to exit at [$Y]. Why not just keep running the business as is? What's the urgency?"

"And is not having clarity on this affecting other areas of your life?"

"When are you wanting to make this happen — this year? Next year?"

"And I know you're saying you want to do this soon — but how committed are you to actually preparing properly? Because most founders I've worked with at EY would tell you the preparation phase is what separates a 4x exit from a 6x exit."

*(The responses here always bring in the emotional drivers — wanting to spend time with family, tired of the grind, health concerns, partner disputes, wanting to start something new, proving it to themselves. There is always a POWERFUL reason behind wanting to exit. Let them tell you.)*

---

## Part Eight — Gaining Commitment & Wrapping in Emotion

"OK [Name], well based on what you've told me, I can definitely help with that."

"Would you like me to tell you about what I do and how it works?"

*(Wait for permission. They should be leaning in at this point.)*

---

## Part Nine — State What You Are an Expert At

"Well, my area of expertise is helping founders and business owners who are preparing for a sale or capital raise to see their business through the buyer's lens — before a buyer does — so they can protect their valuation and control the conversation during due diligence."

"I spent nearly a decade at EY-Parthenon — one of the Big Four — working on the buy side of M&A transactions. My job was to find risk in companies and reduce the price. I've worked on deals worth over $70 billion across Europe, the Middle East, and the US."

"Now I work exclusively with founders — typically businesses doing $2 to $50 million in revenue — who are planning to exit in the next 6 to 18 months. I help them identify exactly what a buyer would find during due diligence that would reduce their valuation, and we fix those issues before they ever go to market."

"Most of the founders I work with tell me this process alone changed how they think about their business — even if they decide not to sell immediately."

*(Then shut up. Let it land. They should be thinking: "That's exactly my situation.")*

---

## Part Ten — State Your Offer & How It Works

*(Wait for them to ask: "So how does that work?" or "What does working with you look like?")*

"It starts with what I call a Sale Readiness Audit. It's a structured assessment across the five areas that buyers evaluate in every deal — financial clarity, operational independence, revenue quality, strategic defensibility, and governance."

"We go through your business together over two to three sessions. I look at it the way a buyer would. At the end, you get a clear picture of where the valuation risks are, what each one could cost you in the final price, and exactly what to fix — with a timeline."

"For founders who want to go deeper, we then work together over two to four months to implement the roadmap — strengthening the business, preparing the data room, and positioning you for the strongest possible outcome when you go to market."

"The goal is simple: when a buyer sits down to evaluate your company, there should be no surprises. You should already know everything they're going to find — because you found it first and fixed it."

*(Keep it high-level. Don't go into granular process details. Keep their brain on the OUTCOME — protecting their valuation — not on features.)*

*(THEN SHUT UP.)*

*(They will start asking questions. Answer each one specifically, then shut up again. Let them build their own picture. When they've asked everything they need to know, they'll ask about price.)*

---

## Part Eleven — State Your Offer with Incentive-Based Pricing

*(This part comes ONLY WHEN they ask "What does it cost?" or "What's the investment?")*

**For the Sale Readiness Audit:**

"The Sale Readiness Audit is $3,500. That covers the full diagnostic — two to three sessions, a comprehensive report with your top valuation risks quantified, and a remediation roadmap."

"But I've found that founders who make decisions quickly always turn out to be the best clients — and we do amazing work together. So I have something called founder pricing: if you make a decision on this call with me today, I knock $500 off and it's just $3,000."

*(Then shut up.)*

**If they ask about the full program:**

"The full Exit Readiness Program — where we implement the roadmap together over two to four months — that's $12,000. But most founders start with the audit to see exactly where they stand, and then we decide together if the full program makes sense."

"With founder pricing on the call today, the audit is $3,000 and it applies as a credit toward the full program if you decide to go deeper."

*(Then shut up.)*

**This silence is the most crucial moment.** You MUST NOT break it. Wait for them to speak — even if it takes 1-2 minutes.

**We're listening for any words that mean YES:**
- "What's the next step?"
- "OK how do we get started?"
- "OK let's do it!"
- "How do I pay?"

**When they're ready:**

"Awesome! Well we can get you started right now if you want. I'll send you a secure payment link and a short intake questionnaire, and we can schedule our first session for this week. Sound good?"

---

## Part Twelve — What We Will Not Accept

Any words that do NOT mean YES or NO. This decision is BINARY.

**Do not accept:**
- "Can you send me something via email?"
- "How do I get back in touch with you?"
- "I need to think about it"
- "I need to talk to my business partner / spouse / CFO"

---

## Objection Handling

### "I need time to think about it."

"I totally get that. Can I ask you — what specifically do you need to think about that we haven't discussed on this call? That's why I take the time to have these conversations. While you still have me here, what questions or concerns are still unanswered?"

*(Discuss those. Then:)*

"Look, the real decision you're making isn't whether or not to work with me. That's really irrelevant. The real decision is whether you're ready to go into your exit prepared — knowing exactly what a buyer will find — or whether you're willing to walk into due diligence and hope for the best. That's the real decision, and it's one you'll face until you're ready to prepare."

---

### "I need to check with my business partner / co-founder / spouse."

"Yes, totally understand. So let me clarify — are you saying you're in, and you just need a sign-off, and then we're moving forward? Or is there something else?"

*(If yes:)* "OK great. When specifically will you be speaking with them?"

"Perfect. So you'll have spoken with your [partner] by [specific time] tomorrow?"

"Great — I'll hold the founder pricing for you until then. Just reach out to me directly and we'll get you enrolled."

*(If needed, offer:)* "Would it be helpful if I got on a quick call with your partner to answer their questions directly? I'm happy to do that."

---

### "I can't afford it right now."

"Tell me more about that."

*(Let them talk.)*

"OK, so you shared with me throughout this call that you're planning to sell a business worth [$X]. We're talking about a $3,000 investment to protect a potential valuation gap of [$hundreds of thousands]. So the question isn't really whether you can afford the audit — it's whether you can afford to go into due diligence without it."

"How do you think a buyer would react if they found the issues we just discussed during their own diligence process?"

---

### "I've worked with consultants before and it didn't help."

"I hear that. And there's somebody you don't trust right now — it's either me or yourself. Which do you think it is, and let's talk about that."

"What specific outcomes would you need to see to make this a complete no-brainer for you?"

*(Listen, then:)* "That's exactly what the audit addresses."

---

### "Can you guarantee results?"

"I can guarantee this: if you go into due diligence unprepared, a buyer will find the issues and use them to reduce your price. That's not a prediction — that's what I did professionally for nearly a decade. The question is whether you find those issues first, or the buyer does."

"Founders who prepare properly don't just get better valuations — they close faster and with fewer deal-killers. I can't guarantee what a buyer will pay, but I can guarantee you'll be in a dramatically stronger position than if you go in blind."

---

### "I'm not planning to sell for another 2-3 years."

"That's actually the ideal time to start. The best exits I've seen at EY were companies that began preparation 12 to 18 months before going to market. The audit gives you a roadmap — you don't have to implement everything at once. But knowing what to fix now means you're building toward a stronger exit every month, not scrambling at the last minute."

---

### The Magic Question (if they're on the fence)

"Look, the real decision you're making isn't whether or not to do an audit with me or anyone else. That's really irrelevant."

"The real decision is whether you're ready to protect the value you've spent [X years] building — or whether you're willing to leave it up to the buyer to decide what your company is worth."

"That's the real decision. And it's one you'll be faced with until you're ready to take control of it."

*(This makes the decision about protecting their life's work — not about buying a service.)*

## FUNNEL FLOW: Ad → VSL → Call → Close

```
AD                          VSL                         CALL
────────────────           ────────────────            ────────────────
Hook: "30% haircut"   →    Educate: 5 drivers     →   Diagnose: their
Fear + curiosity            Build trust: EY story       specific situation
                            Prove pattern: deals        Cause pain: gap
CTA: Watch video            that died                   between desired
or take quiz           →    CTA: Book a call       →    exit and reality

                            VSL does the TEACHING       Call does the
                            (why this matters)          DIAGNOSING
                                                        (what it means
                            Call does NOT re-teach.      for THEM)
                            Prospect arrives
                            already believing            Then: offer +
                            the problem is real.         close
```

**Key principle:** The VSL educates and creates belief. The call diagnoses and creates urgency. Never re-teach on the call what the VSL already covered. The prospect should arrive already believing:
1. Buyers evaluate businesses differently than founders think
2. Valuation haircuts during DD are common and preventable
3. Vaiga has the EY credential and deal experience to help

The call's job is to make it specific to THEIR business and THEIR exit.

## Notes for Vaiga

- **Your EY experience is your closer.** When you say "I spent nearly a decade at EY-Parthenon helping buyers find risk" — that's the most powerful sentence in the entire script. Don't rush it.
- **The silence after stating price is everything.** Practice being comfortable with 60-90 seconds of silence. The first person to speak after price loses.
- **Never apologize for the price.** $3,000 to protect a multi-million dollar exit is absurdly cheap. You know this. They should feel it.
- **Your diagnostic instinct is your superpower.** You don't need to "sell." You need to accurately diagnose their situation. If you do that well, the sale closes itself.
- **Record every call.** Your first 10 calls will teach you more than this script ever could. Listen back. Notice where they lean in. Notice where they hesitate. Iterate.
