Not because the business was bad. Not because the market changed.

When a buyer sees that, they don't think: messy bookkeeping.
They think: what are they hiding?

The seller never saw it coming. They thought they were selling a strong company.
They were. But they weren't prepared for how a buyer would evaluate it.

## [THE CORE PROBLEM]

This is the fundamental disconnect.
Founders build businesses from the inside out. Customers. Products. Growth. Revenue. That's exactly what they should focus on while building.
But buyers evaluate businesses from the outside in.
They look at risk. Transferability. Financial clarity. Operational independence. Customer concentration. Legal exposure.
And every risk a buyer sees becomes a valuation discount.
This is why so many founders are shocked during due diligence.
By the time those issues surface, the negotiation power has already shifted.
The buyer is no longer discovering value.
They're discovering risk. And every risk becomes leverage.

## [THE DATA]

The data reflects this.
More than half of M&A transactions experience a reduction between the initial offer and the final closing price.
In more than fifty percent of deals — the seller receives less than what was originally promised.
Companies without professionally reviewed financials often see valuation discounts of ten to twenty-five percent.
Customer concentration — when a single client represents a large share of revenue — can reduce valuation by another ten to thirty percent.
Heavy dependence on the founder can reduce it further still.
These are not rare edge cases. These are the most common risk factors buyers see every single day.
If a buyer looked at your business tomorrow — how confident are you that your financials would hold up under scrutiny? That your operations could run without you? That no single client represents too large a share of revenue?
If you hesitated on any of those — that hesitation is exactly what a buyer would find during due diligence.

## [THE FIVE DRIVERS — TIGHTENED]

After years of working on transactions, I noticed that buyers consistently evaluate companies across five core areas.
When these are strong, buyers compete. When they contain risk, buyers discount.

**First: Financial clarity.**
Buyers need to trust the numbers. Clean reporting. Consistent accounts. Clear revenue breakdowns.
When financials are inconsistent, buyers assume hidden risk. That alone — as I showed you in the poultry deal — is where the discounts begin and never stop.

**Second: Operational independence.**
Can the business run without the founder?
I worked on a technology deal in Lithuania. Strong product. Serious buyer interested. But every customer relationship, every strategic decision, every piece of institutional knowledge sat with the founder alone. No documented processes. No second-in-command.
The buyer's conclusion was simple: if the founder leaves after acquisition, the business unravels. So they walked.
The founder had built something genuinely valuable. But because that value was locked inside one person instead of embedded in systems and team — it couldn't be transferred. And buyers won't pay for what they can't keep.

**Third: Revenue quality.**
How concentrated is your customer base? How predictable is the revenue?
If thirty or forty percent of revenue comes from a single client, buyers apply an immediate discount. Losing that one client would fundamentally change the business. They price that risk in — whether you're ready for it or not.

**Fourth: Strategic defensibility.**
What makes this business difficult to replicate?
I worked on an acquisition where a large German technology company was buying a smaller software business. The software was the entire reason for the deal — the crown jewel.
During assessment, we discovered the software was built almost entirely on open source code. Freely available. Not proprietary.
The valuation logic collapsed. The buyer walked. The founder had built a real business with real customers. But there was nothing defensible for a buyer to acquire at a premium.

**Fifth: Governance and risk management.**
Legal structure. Compliance. Contracts. Operational discipline.
These determine how predictable and transferable the business truly is.
When all five are strong — buyers compete.
When they're weak — buyers negotiate. And founders lose value they spent years creating.

## [THE SOLUTION]

The founders who achieve the strongest exits do one thing differently.
They prepare before they go to market.
Usually six to eighteen months before a sale or capital raise.
They evaluate their business through the buyer's lens. Identify the risks. Strengthen the key drivers. Build a compelling narrative.
So when buyers begin their evaluation — the company already looks like a high-quality acquisition.
Instead of reacting to diligence questions, the founder controls the conversation.
